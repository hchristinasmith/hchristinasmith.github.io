Problem Solving Reflection

1. Blocked on a Simple Problem

What was the problem? Syntax Error

What problem-solving techniques did you use? Google, looking back in notes, reading the code carefully for misspellings or syntax errors. If a lot of time had gone by, I'd walk away from the computer and come back.

How did you feel throughout the process? Uncomfortable, a little frustrated if I had spent a bit of time on it. Stoked when I figured it out.

What did you learn? To read my code carefully to recognise any spelling mistakes/errors.

2. Elegantly Solved a Problem

What was the problem? A problem on JS Kata.

What problem-solving techniques did you use? Google, looking back in notes, writing out the problem in simple English and trying to translate it into JavaScript. Read syntax carefully.

How did you feel throughout the process? Mostly confused and frustrated, don't think I have elegantly solved a problem yet! But once I got the answer, I felt good.

What did you learn? I should learn to understand how to use console.log more.

3. Reflection on Problem-Solving Techniques
  • Pseudocode: Good - helps me plan before diving into code.
  • Trying something: Good
  • Rubber ducky method: Good - I like to write out the problem in English then try to translate it.
  • Reading error messages: Getting better - messages are still cryptic, but it helps me trace bugs instead of randomly guessing.
  • Console.logging: Not confident - unsure what to console.log, where to place the logs, or how to interpret what I see. I want to get more comfortable using it. Need more practice.
  • Googling: Good - often my go-to.
  • Asking peers for help: Haven't done this so much. I think I will more moving into bootcamp and getting to know people better.
  • Asking coaches for help: I hesitate - trying to balance being resourceful with knowing when to reach out. I feel more confident when I've already tried a few things.
  • Improving process with reflection: Helpful - it helps me recognise patterns, and celebrate progress. I want to keep making time for it, even just a few minutes after a challenge as it strengthens my learning.
4. Reflection on Asking for Help

This happens quite often. Just about every question in the JavaScript Kata I was wanting to ask for help. I didn't want to seem incapable or like I hadn't tried enough. I worried that the question might be “too basic” or that I hadn't earned the right to ask yet. There was also a part of me that felt like asking for help would take away from the satisfaction of figuring it out on my own.

Next time, I want to catch the moment when frustration turns into unproductive looping and use it as a sign to pause and ask. Before reaching out, I can jot down what I've tried so far, what I think the issue is, and what I don't understand. This way, I'm not hustling for an answer, but inviting a conversation that can help me learn. I also want to remind myself that asking for help doesn't make me any less capable.
